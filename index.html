Medicinal and Traditional Uses

Bark

Used in traditional medicine for wound healing and treating ulcers.

Leaves

Applied in folk remedies for diabetes, inflammation, and skin issues.

Flowers

Rich in flavonoids; used for liver support and treating skin diseases.

Traditional Uses

Flowers used for natural dye in Holi; wood used in tools and ritual firewood.

Palash Tree: Environmental Impact

Soil Enrichment

Improves soil fertility by fixing nitrogen, making it ideal for degraded lands.

Drought Resistance

Thrives in dry, poor soils and arid zones, making it suitable for reforestation efforts.

Pollinator Support

Bright flowers attract birds, bees, and insects, boosting local biodiversity and pollination.

Shade Provider

Its deciduous canopy provides seasonal shade to nearby crops, animals, and rural pathways.

Fun Facts and Myths

  • Flame of the Forest: The Palash tree gets its name from its fiery orange-red blossoms that ignite the landscape during spring.
  • Mythical Origin: Believed to have sprouted from the sweat of Lord Shiva and associated with goddess Saraswati in Hindu mythology.
  • Festival Favorite: Palash flowers are traditionally used to make natural colors for Holi and to fuel the sacred Holika Dahan fire.
  • Ancient Warfare: Poison derived from the tree was once used on arrow tips in historical Indian battles.
  • Spring Messenger: The tree's blooming from February to April marks the arrival of spring in many regions of India.
